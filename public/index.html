Instructions to Cancelers

  1. Corporate PACs put real money into federal elections. The figures on this ledger are public record. Money, however, cannot enter a voting booth. You can. That asymmetry is the entire mechanism.
  2. At the Bureau's official exchange rate, one (1) vote is worth $100 of political money. To pledge yours against a line item, completely fill in the oval beside it. Use ink. Arithmetic is an acceptable ink.
  3. Each additional stamp deposits another canceler's worth of intent, $100 at a time. When a PAC's balance hits zero it is declared OVERDRAWN, a condition with no legal meaning and enormous moral clarity.
  4. Your deposit remains PENDING until you cast an actual ballot. A pledge without a ballot is a bounced check.

      Part 3 — Notes on methodology

      ¹ On the exchange rate

      OpenSecrets puts the 2024 federal cycle at $15.9 billion; about 155 million ballots were cast. That's $102.43 of political money per vote, certified down to an even $100. Regional rates exist: the 2022 midterm discount (~$79) and the Georgia-runoff premium (~$111). The Bureau quotes the round number because the Bureau does not make change.

      ² On the allegation

      We do not allege corruption. We allege arithmetic. Every figure on the ledger is a public FEC record of lawful contributions, rounded to the nearest $100,000. The companies did the disclosing; we did the dividing.

      ³ On method

      This site will never tell you whom to vote for or against — that's your homework, and better researchers than us grade it. Start with the Integrity Index (who takes corporate PAC money and trades stocks in office) and OpenSecrets (who funds whom). Neither is affiliated with this site. Yet.

      ⁴ On follow-through

      A pledge here is a birthday wish until you vote. Registration takes two minutes at vote.gov. Make a plan — when, where, how you're getting there. Voters with a plan actually go. That's the part of this that isn't a joke.

      The fine print. This is satire with a turnout problem to solve. Company and PAC names appear solely for commentary on public campaign-finance records; no company endorses, sponsors, or has anything to do with this site, and no logos or trademarks are used. Figures are lawful, disclosed contributions per FEC filings, rounded, as of the date in our data file — we allege arithmetic, not crimes. Votes don't literally cancel dollars; they do something better: they count. No prizes, no rewards, no merch. We store a counter and, if you offer one, a first name. Nothing else.
